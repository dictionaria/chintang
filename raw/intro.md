<h3 id="A dictionary of Chintang">&nbsp;</h3>
<h3 id="A dictionary of Chintang">A dictionary of Chintang&nbsp;</h3>
<h3 id="Introduction">1. Introduction</h3>
<p>This trilingual dictionary can best be considered as the linguistic landscape of the Chintang area, a trilingual Kiranti community from Eastern Nepal. Along with native Chintang words, this dictionary contains numerous Nepali and Bantawa entries, which reflect the complex linguistic reality of this community. This publication, which has largely grown out of the Chintang corpus, provides the most comprehensive insight into the treasure of the Chintang language, for it encompasses a wide array of domains and semantic fields in addition to many translated examples. Therefore, it is a unique tool with an immense value for the documentation of the Kiranti languages and the study of multilingualism and linguistics in general.</p>
<h3 id="The Chintang language">1.1 The Chintang language</h3>
<p>Chintang ([ˈt̻s̻ʰiɳʈaŋ], ISO 639-3 ctn) is a Kiranti language spoken by approximately 4,000&ndash;5,000 speakers in the Dhankuta district of Eastern Nepal. The name of the language is also the name of the area where the language is spoken[^2]. In Nepali it is commonly referred to as Chintāṅge Bhāṣā, literally translated as &lsquo;Chintangish language&rsquo;. Apart from the Chintang area and neighboring Ahale, the language is spoken by a considerable number of people who periodically or permanently leave the Chintang area and migrate Dhankuta and Kathmandu (Nepal's capital city).<br />
Chintang is an endangered language. Despite still serving as the principal medium of communication among adults, Nepali, the national language of Nepal, has inexorably made its way up to the Kiranti hills. In the last ten years, there has been a widespread shift to using Nepali as the main language to speak to children and Chintang is therefore no longer acquired by most children. A similar pattern can be observed in neighboring languages and may be connected to a nation-wide phenomenon of language loss.<br />
As a Kiranti language, Chintang is part of the large Tibeto-Burman (Sino-Tibetan) family. Its closest relatives within Kiranti are the neighboring languages Chulung (Chɨlɨŋ), Belhare and Athpare. Another neighboring language, Bantawa, belongs to a different branch of Kiranti but it is in intensive contact with Chintang.&nbsp;Most speakers of Chintang are&nbsp;therefore trilingual, also being fluent in Bantawa and Nepali.</p>

<h3 id="Society and economy">1.2 Society and economy</h3>
<p>The speakers of Chintang identify themselves as <em>Rai</em> (Rāī), which is a ethnonym that refers to groups in Eastern Nepal, West Bengal and Sikkim in India. These people speak, or have spoken, a Kiranti language and also share the cultural and oral tradition of mundum. (Rai et al., 2009; Gaenszle 2016, 327f.). The Rai identity overlaps with village or clan conceptions although a Chintang-specific identity, especially in local politics, may be a valid currency amid the context of ethnic awareness that runs through modern Nepal in addition to the impact that the documentation project of Chintang has had on its speakers. In the Nepalese context, Rai stands as one of the many ethnic groups of the country. Furthermore, <em>Rai</em> (Rāī) is the surname of many members of the different Rai groups, as is customary in Nepal.<br />
The name Chintang has only recently become an ethnic category, as it turned out that the original inhabitants of Chintang have a distinct Rai (Kiranti) language. However, in a localizing sense the adjectival term Chintange is applied to all residents of the village, including various other Rai (mainly Bantawa, but also Puma, Kulung, Thulung etc.) as well as Chetri (Kṣētrī) or Kami (Kāmī). A closer look at history and social organization shows that there are "original settlers", the Chintange proper, who have a distinct language, and only in later times other Rai, especially Bantawa from the Bhojpur district (across the Arun river), have immigrated and settled alongside these Chintang. They have intermarried and often adopted the Chintang language as a first or at least second language, yet in terms of patrilineal clans there is still a clear distinction between ethnic Chintange and localized Chintange. The original Chintang clans do not claim to have a common ancestor. This is a somewhat unusual situation, as many Rai groups have a mythic forefather, and the myths generally describe how his offspring settled and founded the different clans. According to local myth (in fact, different and loosely linked stories), the first forefathers who settled in present day's Chintang were <em>Sambhoŋ, Chentaŋhaŋpa, Rahaŋba</em> and <em>Khɨkkhaŋ</em>. Thus the Chintang clans are derived from no less than four different forefathers, but in the narratives it is not clear where the forefathers came from. They may well have come from different regions.
<br />There is however one mythic ancestor, called <em>Budhahaŋ</em>, who is an important figure in local thinking: Budhohang (along with Rajdeu) is worshiped on almost all major ritual celebrations. Budhahang is a kind of local hero, who fought against the Shah king, using magical powers which made him successful initially, though he was later vanquished and disappeared mysteriously. However, the people believe that he is still alive and immortal. The story is consistent in some details about a son who was murdered, and about another person (who was of Brahman descent) whom he adopted. It is possible that some of the elements of the story have a historical base. Well known beyond the village is the temple of the goddess Chintang Devi. Many visitors from various groups (including high castes) journey there on a pilgrimage. In fact, the temple is en route to the ancient pilgrimage site of Varāhakshetra down at the confluence of Saptakośī and it seems that many generations of Kirantis traveled down to the Tarai walked along this major path (adapted from Gaenszle & Lieven 2013; Gaenszle et al. 2013, 100f.; Gaenszle et al. 2005, 33f.; Rai et al. 2009, 24f.).</p>

<p>Most inhabitants of Chintang practice subsistence agriculture with a focus on crops like millet, maize and rice as well as <em>cash crops</em> such as tomatoes. Most households also own livestock such as goats, cows, chicken and pigs. Land is highly atomized in Chintang and most households have one or more lots in the area. Farming and animal husbandry is complemented by sporadically foraging the woods for edible roots and yams and by growing other, less profitable crops such as chili, ginger and other vegetables. A relatively new development in the local economy was the large-scale introduction of mandarin orange trees, the fruit of which is sold in Hile and Dhankuta. The aforementioned Chintang temple has also enabled a small-scale source of income for local farmers who sell fruits at the temple's entrance. Furthermore, the high number of devotees has bettered the accessibility between Chintang, Dhankuta and Dharan.</p>

<h3 id="Sources  of the dictionary, documentation project and publications">2. Sources of the dictionary, documentation project and publications</h3>
<p>The Chintang dictionary is largely derived from the Chintang corpus[^2] plus specific elicitation. In this way the present dictionary differs from other lexicographic publications in how words were collected, as the transcription from everyday speech overweights linguistic elicitation in number. The Chintang Language Corpus is broadly divided into two sections: child speech acquisition and adult corpus. The language acquisition corpus focuses on six children from the age of two to four years. Most recording sessions in this subcorpus are unsupervised. For this reason, the language acquisition corpus not only contains child speech but a roughly equal amount of natural adult speech as well.<br /> The other subcorpus is the (adult) linguistic corpus. Here, most recording sessions were supervised, sometimes with specific instructions that aimed at raising the use of grammatical phenomena of interest (cf. Schikowski 2013b). This subcorpus contains various genres:<br /><br /> &bull; conversation: two or more people talking to each other, often with a given topic<br /> &bull; description: objective description of things and processes; also contains special subgenres such as cooking recipes <br /> &bull; experimental: Pear Stories, Frog Stories, and elicitation with picture cards<br /> &bull; narrative: biographical and historical narratives as well as folk tales<br /> &bull; ritual language: language accompanying rituals, mostly monologues in the distinct mundum register<br /> &bull; songs: all kinds of songs, mostly sung and written by the same person<br /> <br /> The corpus is glossed and has served the linguistic research on Chintang and linguistics in general. There are publications on the language's deictic system (Dirksmeyer, 2008), interchangeability of prefixes (Bickel et al., 2007), object-conditioned differential marking (Schikowski, 2013a) as well as studies on language acquisition (Stoll et al., 2011). Turning to lexicography, the documentation project produced one community dictionary that was conceived for native speakers and learners of the language and was distributed in printed form in the village. An online version can be found <a href="http://www.clrp.uzh.ch/downloads/ctn-dict.pdf/">here</a>.<br /> During 2020-22, the dictionary was revised and sorted for its present publication. This endeavor included sorting out faulty or otherwise obscure words and consulting native speakers about entries where there were doubts. Elicitation in this part was carried out online due to the pandemic. </p>

<h3 id="Conventions and usage of the dictionary">3. Conventions and usage of the dictionary</h3>
<p>This section will address issues regarding how to navigate through the dictionary. It covers the most recurrent abbreviations and how to interpret the entries, especially regarding the verbs. The orthography will be described in section 3.4.</p>
<h4 id="Abbreviations">3.1 Abbreviations</h4>
<p>This section provides a list of abbreviations found in the dictionary.</p>
<p><strong>lex</strong> - <em>lex</em> gives the lemma; the lexical entry as it is found on the dictionary. The entries in the dictionary are arranged alphabetically based on this form. For verbs, it can be thought of as the stem. Since Chintang has stem alternation (cf. section 4.2), this form is preferred over the infinitive <em>-ma</em> (<strong>ct</strong> citation form),&nbsp;as phonotactical considerations may conceal crucial differences in the stem. Cf. <em>lap-</em> 'catch' and <em>lapt-</em> 'catch.for.somebody', both with the same infinitive <em>lapma</em>. For many tetrasyllabic adverbs this slot also indicates their citation form with the
adverbializer <em>-wa</em>.</p>

<p><strong>alt</strong> - Some entries have an alternative pronunciation.</p>

<p><strong>morph</strong> - This field indicates all regular allomorphs.</p>

<p><strong>ps</strong> - This provides the part of speech of the entry. These are also displayed as abbreviations and their list can be found in the following section (3.2).</p>

<p><strong>val</strong> - This indicates the valency of verbs. A fuller account of the valency patterns present in Chintang can be found in section&nbsp;4.5.</p>
<p><strong>lg</strong> - This indicates the origin of the lexeme. C stands for Chintang, B for Bantawa, N for Nepali, E for English and H for Hindi. The combined form C/N stands for Nepali loanwords that have undergone unpredictable phonological assimilation or semantic change, such as <em>ubheni</em> 'morning' from Nepali <em>bihāna</em> or <em>sojje</em> 'in.vain' (probably from N. <em>sojho</em> 'straight'). Furthermore, C/N also includes compounds with both Chintang and Nepali elements, names of places and people where the ultimate etymological origin is unclear, old Indo-Aryan loanwords that have retained an archaic phonology in addition to adverbs, sounds and ideophones that cannot unproblematically be assigned to a particular language. Similarly, in the case of Bantawa loanwords, the large stock of shared vocabulary added to the long-standing contact between these languages often blurs the line between them. Suspected Bantawa loanwords and Bantawa-Chintang compounds are marked with B/C. The reader is advised that larger Kiranti comparative research is still warranted.</p>

<p><strong>ety</strong> - For a small set of entries, mostly compounds, there is an etymological indication.
</p>
<p><strong>de/gn</strong> - This stands for the definition of the entry for all parts of speech in English and Nepali respectively. Some entries have more than one definition. The Nepali definition <strong>gn</strong> is written in the Devanagari script. <br />
For some sounds, interjections and adverbs there are some cases where the definition is given as <em>ideophone</em> with a larger explanation under <strong><em>seme</em></strong> (if available), this means that the precise meaning is either deduced from the context of the utterance or because we lack a good equivalent in English that would capture the whole meaning in one word, as in <em>hopi</em> 'ideophone; to respire deeply while dying'.</p>
<p><strong>seme/semn</strong> - For some entries there is an expanded definition which is more precise than <strong>de/gn</strong>.
</p>
<p><strong>se/des/gns</strong> - For some entries, mostly verbs, there is further meaning derived from their combination with another element, usually a noun, with varying degrees of lexicalization, e.g.: <em>papa thokma</em> 'be.sinful', from <em>thokma</em> 'hit, bite'.
</p>
<p><strong>ex/exe/exn/exsrc</strong> - These stand for the examples in Chintang, in English, in Nepali and their source respectively. Most examples come from the Chintang Corpus (described in section 2.) and they can be identified by their tag, which corresponds to the nomenclature of the corpus. Some examples have been adapted from the aforementioned community dictionary.</p>
<p><strong>ct</strong> - This indicates the citation form. For verbs it is the infinitive <em> -ma</em> in Chintang and <em>-nu</em> in Nepali. This slot also includes a genera of fixed noun combinations - termed 'binomials' - that are most prominent in the <em>mundum</em> register (cf. Gaenszle et al. 2012).</p>
<p><strong>reg</strong> - This marker is only found in entries that correspond to a particular register. The most recurrent register in this dictionary is <em>ritual</em>, which stands for words that are specific to the religious register known as <em>mundum</em> (cf. Rai et al. 2009) plus words that refer to the spiritual dimension of life. Entries that are only found in childspeech (as either uttered by children or adults speaking to children) are marked as <em>child</em>. Words that are deemed to be abusive by speakers have the marker <em>abusive</em>. The small set of words that are only found in Sambugaũ dialect (mostly borrowed from Bantawa) are indicated by the name of the village.</p>
<p><strong>com</strong> - For some entries there are comments of various types, such as indications on morpho-phonology, relation to other entries and remarks provided by informants.
</p>
<p><strong>qst</strong> - The present dictionary has largely grown out of a corpus, as indicated above. This vast project allows for a deep insight into the language; but the vast size of the dictionary also includes several entries where the authors accept doubt, i.e. an uncertainty about the semantic scope, the origin of an entry, the exact valency pattern of a verb, etc. Such questions are expressed in this slot.
</p>

<h4 id="Part of speech POS">3.2 Part of speech POS</h4>
<p>This section provides a list with all abbreviations of part of speech (POS) found in the present dictionary.</p>
<p><strong>adj</strong> - The label adjectives in Chintang amalgamates three categories: native adjectives, predicate nouns and Nepali adjectives. The first category has only two members (<em>mi-</em> and <em>the-</em>) and they must occur with a nominalizer. Predicate nouns, such as <em>umaŋ</em> 'raw' or <em>beŋthe</em> 'angry' can appear as arguments of verbs such as <em>lis-</em> 'be' and <em>yuŋs-</em> 'be there' as nouns would. However, they differ from nouns in their inability to both take case inflection and to occur in any other argument position that would require a case other than the nominative. Lastly, adjectives have been profusely borrowed from Nepali and often replace indigenous Chintang stative verbs that have an equivalent meaning, as in <em>aptharo</em> (from N.) 'difficult' for native <em>chakt-</em> 'be.difficult'.</p>

<p><strong>adv</strong> - Adverbs form an independent class that does not allow inflection and modifies predicates or whole clauses. Chintang has a large number of adverbs composed of duplicated and triplicated roots (termed ideophones, cf. Rai et al. 2005) with a variety of meanings such as: <em>tenenewa</em> 'manner of shouting' and <em>sololowa</em> 'the sound of something big moving close to the ground, e.g. a big snake or also a river in its bed'.</p>
<p><strong>gm</strong> - The term grammatical markers encompasses an ample number of heterogeneous suffixes and unbound words that carry semantic and grammatical information. These include clitics, morphology found in verbs, nouns and pronouns, particles, certain locatives, nominalizers, classifiers borrowed from Nepali and unbound topic markers.</p>
<p><strong>interj</strong> - Interjections are independent lexemes that express a reaction or a feeling. They are uninflected and do not interact with other elements, hence building a clause of their own. An example would be <em>hauk</em> 'used to keep somebody (particularly children) from doing or touching something'.</p>
<p><strong>n</strong> - Nouns are independent lexemes that stand for referents. Nouns differ from other parts of speech in that they are inflected for number, case and possession. Chintang has borrowed many of nouns from Nepali.</p>

<p><strong>pro</strong> - The label pronoun stands for lexemes that can replace a noun or noun phrase. Based on their morphosyntactic behavior we can distinguish three different groups: a) deictic references to 3 person (demonstratives), b) speech act participants, the only group that can be inflected for clusivity (for 1st person only) and c) question words <em>sa-</em> 'who' and <em>them</em> 'what' which share their complete morphosyntax (including case, number, and possession) with nouns but are similar to deictics in their mode of referring.</p>

<p><strong>sound</strong> - Sound stands for an ample term that includes onomatopoetic or otherwise descriptive sounds. Sounds are distinguished from interjections and ideophones in that the former require a citative <em>-mo</em> .</p>

<p><strong>vi/vt</strong> - The Chintang dictionary distinguishes between intransitive (vi) and transitive (vt) verbs. This is also consistently made for verb pairs with an identical stem that would correspond to syntactic alternations, such as <em>dipt</em> (vi) 'be.covered' and <em>dipt</em> (vt) 'cover'. This is a property of the language that has been termed <em>flexible valency </em> (cf. Schikowski et al. 2015) and will be discussed in section 4.5.<br />
Verbs are inflected for tense, aspect, modus polarity and display agreement with S, A and/or O in number, tense and clusivity. They form a closed class and there is no morphological process to make new verbs. Verbs borrowed from Nepali take a nativizer <em>-e</em> and are expressed with an auxiliary, as in: <em>khic-e num-ma </em> [record-nativizer do-infinitive] 'to record, to make pictures'.<br />
Some verbs include a preverbal element. Although these form a single lexeme, endoclitics and prefixes hosted by the verb may occur between preverb and the verbal stem. Most prefixes seem to have their origin in nouns.</p>

<p><strong>v2</strong> - v2 stands for vector verbs, i.e. concatenations of stems. These are always recruited from verbs and may affect&nbsp;the host verb by altering its aspectual reading, valency or by adding a further noun phrase to the clause (e.g. <em> steal and eat </em>). Vector verbs can host prefixes and suffixes and they impose their own agreement morphology and even their own tense morphology in some cases (Bickel &amp; Z&uacute;&ntilde;iga 2017, 177f.). Vector verbs will be described in further detail in section 4.4.&nbsp;</p>

<h4 id="Abbreviations">3.3 Dialectal variation</h4>

<p>Chintang presents a low degree of dialectal variation which is concentrated on its two main villages: Mulgaũ and Sambugaũ. In terms of lexical difference, apart from isolated words, the most prominent feature is the influence of Bantawa in parts of Sambugaũ. For example, speakers of Sambugaũ might say <em>yɨŋ</em> 'language' (cf. Bantawa <em>yɨŋ</em> 'language') instead of <em> rɨŋ</em>. Conversely, this common Kiranti stock may help to preserve original words, as it is the case with <em>piʔ</em> for 'cow' (in both Chintang and local Bantawa), which is common in Sambugaũ but perceived as old-fashioned in Mulgaũ by some speakers who may prefer the Nepali word <em>gāī</em>.<br /> In terms of morphology there are two issues that should be mentioned. The first is the prefix <em>kha-</em> [1nsO] in Mulgaũ that corresponds to the forms <em>ma-</em> [1nsOe] and <em>mai-</em> [1nsOi] in Sambugaũ in the transitive verbal paradigms. Hence, the latter variant has been taken as the standard in the present work. The second difference worth noting is the negative past marker <em>-yokt</em>, which only occurs in parts of Mulgaũ. Everywhere else <em>-th</em> prevails, a suffix which further marks the negative imperative in all dialects.</p>

<li>There is an on-going change which merges initial velar nasal [ŋ-] with its alveolar counterpart [n-]. Hence <em>ŋa</em> 'fish' is considered an old-fashioned pronunciation.</li>
<li>The voiced retroflex plosive [ɖ] &lt;d&gt; is sometimes produced as an alveolar flap [ɾ] between vowels. This&nbsp;is also the case in Bantawa.</li>
<li>Aspirated voiceless velar plosive [kʰ]&nbsp;alternates freely with voiceless fricative velar [x], a phenomenon also found in Nepali.</li>
<li>Aspirated voiceless bilabial plosive [pʰ] alternates freely with fricative bilabial [ɸ],&nbsp;a phenomenon also found in Nepali.</li>
<li>Aspirated voiced bilabial plosive [bh] is rare in original Chintang words and it is mostly found in loanwords from Nepali.</li>
</ul>



<h4 id="Limits of the dictionary">3.5 Limits of the dictionary</h4>
<p>The present dictionary incorporates an immense number of entries that encompass most aspects of the language. However vast the range of words in this dictionary is, it cannot be conclusive. As mentioned in section 2. the present dictionary is mostly based on a corpus and not on systematic lexicographic elicitation. Even though the number of entries is immense, especially for a minority language, and the possibility of extracting vocabulary from recordings of various registers and different levels of supervision is an asset, specific vocabulary and rare words may be lacking in some cases. This is especially the case for wild fauna and flora and other semantic fields that rarely occur in everyday speech. Furthermore, having a camera and a recording device might have hindered the use of swear words or other registers deemed less appropriate for a recorded setting. In this line, we can also refer to the case of religious terminology, the mundum register; the precise meaning of some of the mundum entries remains obscure mainly due to their spiritual character, which often escapes simple translations. Furthermore, the mundum register is only known to mundum experts and not to the layman. Finding a precise description of these words will require a larger ethnographic study (cf. Rai et al 2009, 31f.).<br />
A further issue is the variety of Nepali language that is spoken in Chintang. Chintang has borrowed extensively from Nepali, particularly nouns. Aditionally, code switching is also very common in corpus recording, which explains the high number of entries of Nepali origin from all parts of speech. Here the reader must be warned about the quality of those Nepali words; in most cases, the type of Nepali that was recorded during the creation of the corpus corresponds to what we might call <em>Chintang</em> (or <em>Kiranti</em>) Nepali. In practical terms, this means that some phonological features of Nepali are neutralized, much in line with Chintang phonology. Most importantly we can mention the Nepali vowel /ʌ/ &lt;a&gt; that merges with /a/ &lt;ā&gt; and the series of dental consonants that merge with their retroflex counterparts. Thus, we find in the dictionary two entries of Nepali origin under <em>dhan</em>: one meaning 'wealth' in N. <em>dhan</em> /dhʌn/ and the other 'growing.rice' in N. <em>dhān</em> /dhan/ (note the different quality of the vowels, both are pronounced [ɖʰan] in Chintang). Furthermore, under <em>tin</em> we find both 'tin' (an English loanword that entered Chintang through Nepali) N. <em>ṭin</em> /ʈin/ and 'three' (from N.) that is pronounced in Nepali as <em>tin</em> /t̪in/ (note the difference on the initial stop, both are pronounced [ʈin] in Chintang). In conclusion, the entries of Nepali origin (marked by a N) must be considered in relation to Chintang or Chintang Nepali at best, as they do not match the standard of a lexicographic study on their own. </p>



<h3 id="Grammar">4. Selected issues of the Chintang grammar</h3>
<p>This section will address several aspects of the grammar of Chintang that are deemed relevant for the&nbsp;use of the dictionary or are generally unavoidable for developing an understanding of the language. The topics are: stem alternation, interchangeability of prefixes, compound verbs and valency patterns. This section will start with a typological introduction to the language.</p>

<h4>4.1 A typological introduction to the language</h4>
<p>The typological profile of Chintang can be largely derived from its embedding into the Kiranti family and predominantly its Eastern branch. Phonologically, Chintang is inconspicuous: it has an average-sized phoneme inventory (6 vowels, 25 consonants) with the four-way contrast in stops typical of the whole South Asian region and the syllable structure (C)V(C). Chintang is, however, unusual by South (and Central) Asian standards when it comes to morphology and syntax. The language has a high degree of syntheticity (10 morphemes and more per word) and a wealth of morphological categories: nouns mark case, number, and possession (which includes clusivity), verbs index person, number, and clusivity of one or two arguments (depending on verb class and valency manipulation) and mark tense, mood, aspect, and polarity.<br />
Both nouns and verbs have a complex morphological structure. There are 16 morphological cases (among them rare ones such as 
altitudinal locatives), many of which can be stacked to form composite meanings. Verbs have both prefixes and 
suffixes, with quite different properties. Prefixes are special in that they can be freely ordered and can host 
endoclitics. An interesting morphological subsystem apart from nouns and verbs is 
presented by the small group of deictics. These words mark the relative location of an object including its altitude, 
its distance from the speaker, and subjectively perceived departures from standard distances.<br />
The &ldquo;basic&rdquo; alignment pattern of Chintang is ergative, but this varies greatly across constructions and verb classes. Besides the morphological argument selectors of case and agreement, there are also many syntactic environments that treat one group of arguments differently from another, e.g. S/O detranzitivisation, coreference constraints for non-finite forms, and marginal agreement in non-finite forms. There is a large number of mechanisms available for clause joining. Relative clauses are expressed by participles or polyfunctional nominalizers, which can also form complement clauses. A diverse set of forms, including converbs and conjunctions, are used for adverbial subordination. Among the most complex forms used in subordination is the infinitive, which goes hand in hand with exceptional case marking and various kinds of long distance agreement (CLRP 2013).</p>

<h4 id="Stem alternation">4.2 Stem alternation</h4>
<p>Stem alternation is an overarching term that&nbsp;stands for changes in the verbal stem. In Chintang, it refers to the phonotactical interaction between the stem and a suffix&nbsp;in which the&nbsp;former undergoes a change based on the following sound, in this case, the next suffix's&nbsp;initial sound. It is therefore a purely phonological process with no effect on tempus or aspect. Stem alternation is a pervasive phenomenon in the Kiranti language family (cf. Herce 2020) and it is also present in several Tibeto-Burman varieties (cf. Jacques 2012).<br />

<h4 id="Free prefix ordering">4.3 Free prefix ordering</h4>
<p>The morphology of verbs in Chintang can be divided into prefixes (pre-domain) and suffixes (post-domain) since different rules apply to each. In this section, we will delineate a property of the predomain in which affixes can be interchanged without affecting the semantic or morphosyntactic structure of the predicate. <br /> Free morpheme permutation is a rare phenomenon in the word's language and, when it is found, it mostly involves clitics. In the case of Chintang, Bickel et al. (2007) have demonstrated that prefixes are not clitics and that they form a grammatical word with the host verb. Nevertheless, the permutation of prefixes does not seem to affect information structure or semantics nor is it mediated by any dialect, sociolect, or idiolect choice. Let us look at one example:</p>
<div class="align-me">(5)
<div>a.&emsp;u-kha-ma-cop-yokt-e</div>
<div>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&emsp;3<small>NS.</small>A-1<small>NS.</small>P-<small>NEG</small>-see-<small>NEG-PST</small></div>
<br />
<div>b.&emsp;u-ma-kha-cop-yokt-e</div>
<div>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&emsp;3<small>NS.</small>A-<small>NEG</small>-1<small>NS.</small>P-see-<small>NEG-PST</small></div>
<br />
<div>c.&emsp;kha-u-ma-cop-yokt-e</div>
<div>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&emsp;1<small>NS.</small>P-3<small>NS.</small>A-<small>NEG</small>-see-<small>NEG-PST</small></div>
<br />
<div>d.&emsp;ma-u-kha-cop-yokt-e</div>
<div>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&emsp;<small>NEG</small>-3<small>NS.</small>A-1<small>NS.</small>P-see-<small>NEG-PST</small></div>
<br />
<div>e.&emsp;kha-ma-u-cop-yokt-e</div>
<div>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&emsp;1<small>NS.</small>P-<small>NEG</small>-3<small>NS.</small>A-see-<small>NEG-PST</small></div>
<br />
<div>f.&emsp;ma-kha-u-cop-yokt-e</div>
<div>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&emsp;<small>NEG</small>-1<small>NS.</small>P-3<small>NS.</small>A-see-<small>NEG-PST</small></div>
<br /> &nbsp;&nbsp;&emsp;All: &lsquo;They didn&rsquo;t see us.&rsquo;</div>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>In this list of examples, we observe&nbsp;all possible combinations in a complex verb with three prefixes. This phenomenon is explained by a prosodic subcategorization in which each prefix and each stem projects a phonological word. Each projected word can carry a prefix at any position.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>



<h4 id="Complex predicates">4.4 Compound verbs</h4>
<p>Multiverb constructions are present in all members of the Kiranti language family. According to Doornenbal (2009, 473f.), they possess enough similarities to be described under one single term: Kiranti compound verbs. The grammaticalized verbs employed in this construction are also known as: vector verbs, aspectalizers or v2s. For the author, who has written&nbsp;a comparative analysis of multiverb constructions in this language family, Kiranti compound verbs are an asymmetrical sequence of two or more verbs where the first verb adds both the main semantic content and the argument structure while the following verb or verbs adjust the meaning by providing additional information (such as valency or aspect). <br />The Chintang compound verb is very similar to the cognate constructions in the Kiranti family. They are complex verbs in which each element shares part of the final suffixal chain on verb# (the final verb). All affixes apply to the whole compound even if they are not present on all elements.&nbsp;<br />According to Bickel &amp; Z&uacute;&ntilde;iga (2017, 176f.) v2 stems select not only a general class of verbs but impose various constraints on valency and Aktionsart specific to each v2. Some v2s specify whether they combine with monovalent or polyvalent verbs. Other v2 stems combine with verbs of any valency, e.g. <em>-loĩs</em> &lsquo;move out&rsquo; combines both with mono-valent and bivalent predicates (whereas closely related <em>-lond</em> &lsquo;go out&rsquo; seems to be limited to monovalent stems).&nbsp;Such differences are not predictable, and they thus represent selection properties intrinsic to each v2.<br />V2 stems behave morphologically like other verb stems, except that they require a two-syllable template as their host. This template consists of a verb stem and as much finite verb morphology as is needed to fulfill the two-syllable constraint (Bickel et al. 2007, 49f.). &nbsp;If there is no suitable inflectional material available, as happens to be the case for example in third person subjunctive forms, a dummy syllable <em>na</em> is inserted in order to meet the two-syllable constraint. This is&nbsp;displayed in (1), where the v2 stem <em>ca</em>- denotes a kind of self-benefaction:</p>
<div class="align-me">(1)
<div>mai-met-th-a, joso-ta num-na-<strong>ca</strong>-ne-na</div>
<div><small>NEG</small>-do-<small>NEG-IMP</small>[2sS] whatever-<small>FOC</small> do-<small>NA</small>-<strong><small>v2</small>:enjoy.for.oneself</strong>[3sS.<small>SBJV]-OPT-INSIST</small></div>
<div>'Don't do that, let her do whatever she wants on her own.' [CLLDCh1R02S04.0781]<br /><br /></div>
</div>
<div class="align-me">(2)
<div>jo-go-yaŋ, na-khutt-i-<strong>ca</strong>-i-<strong>hatt</strong>-i-<strong>bid</strong>-i</div>
<div>whatever-<small>NMLZ-ADD</small> 3[S]&gt;2-steal-2pO-<strong><small>v2</small>:eat</strong>2pO-<strong><small>v2</small>:move.away.<small>TR</small></strong>-2pO-<strong><small>v2</small>:do.for</strong>-[SBJV]2pO</div>
</div>
<div>'It (a cat) may steal everything from you and eat it all up!' [story.cat.204]</div>
<p>&nbsp;</p>

<p>Some v2 elements control the agreement morphology of their V1 (e.g. those with a causative or benefactive meaning component like in (2)) and temporal-aspectual v2s not only control their V1's tense morphology but come  close to being tempus-aspect markers such as <em>-a</em> ‘<small>PST</small>’ or <em>-nok</em> <small>‘IND.NPST’</small> (Schikowski 2022 p.c.). Other v2s follow the agreement and TAMP pattern of their host.<br />Most v2s bear etymological resemblance to regular, non-selecting verb stems, and it is sometimes unclear whether there is one morpheme used in two ways or two morphemes. Compare the use of <em>thand</em>- as a v2 in (3) and as an independent verb in (4):</p>

<div class="align-me">(3)
<div>ba som-ce-ta a-<strong>thand</strong>-u-ce haŋ iss-a-kt-e ?</div>
<div><small>DEM.PROX</small> sort-ns-<small>FOC</small> 2[s]s-<strong>move.down</strong>-3O-3nsO-<small>[SBJV]</small> if be.not.good- <small>PST-IPFV-IND.PST</small></div>
<div>'Wouldn't it be good if you brought down this sort of (stuff)?' [CLLDCh3R12S04.448]</div>
<div>&nbsp;</div>
<div class="align-me">(4)
<div>yo dhukkhur-a apt-u-<strong>thand</strong>-u-ku-ŋ paĩ</div>
<div><small>DEM.ACROSS</small>&nbsp;dove-<small>NTVZ</small> shoot-3[s]O-<strong>v2:move.down.<small>TR</small></strong>-3[s]O-<small>IND.PST</small>-1sA today</div>
<div>'I'll shoot that dove over there down today.' [CLLDCh3R05S05.303]</div>
<p>&nbsp;</p>

<p>Similar examples can be found with several other v2 stems. For example, <em>ca</em>- has an abstract meaning in (1) but a very concrete one of eating in (2). In the concrete meaning, <em>ca</em>- is a regular stem that can also be used as such (e.g. <em>ca!</em> 'eat!'). Whether or not speakers have acquired a generalized representation or a dense association network covering all such usages is an open issue. There may be variation across individuals. From a diachronic point of view, it seems clear that v2 stems originate from regular verb stems and have gradually acquired the selection properties of v2 stems.</p>





<h4 id="Valency patterns">4.5 Valency patterns</h4>
<p>
The description of valency in Chintang concerns the number of arguments of a verb, the semantic roles occupied by these arguments, the way morphosyntactic markers such as case, agreement or even word order are linked to these arguments and how these patterns are determined by lexical classes (cf. Schikowski et al. 2015).<br/>
The frame is a central concept in the analysis of valency: it is defined as a specific type of argument realization, as established by case marking and agreement. In describing frames, the following formalism (adopted from the database used in the Leipzig Valency Classes Project, Hartmann et al. 2012 also cf. Bickel 2011a) will be used:</p>
<ul>
<li>predicate with set of core roles X, Y: {X Y V} (e.g. {S V}: intransitive frame)</li>
<li>role X marked by case C: X-C (e.g. T-NOM: T marked by nominative)</li>
<li>roles X, Y associated with agreement marker sets a and b: V-a(X).b(Y) (e.g. {V-a(S).o(3s)}: S linked to agreement marker set &lsquo;a&rsquo;, dummy third person singular in agreement marker set &lsquo;o&rsquo;)</li>
</ul>

<p>Frames are basically lexically determined and then modified by syntactic factors. A list of all valency patterns found in Chintang is available at the <a href="http://valpal.info/languages/chintang">ValPal database</a>.
In this dictionary, the valency pattern is indicated for most verbs under the abbreviation <strong>val</strong>. We shall describe the three most common valency patterns found in the corpus:</p>

<ul>
<li>Intransitive frame {S-NOM V-s(S)}. This is the most common frame in the language. The verb only agrees with the Subject, which occurs in the nominative (unmarked).
</li>
<li>Monotransitive frame {A-ERG P-NOM V-a(A).o(P)}. The verb displays a bipersonal agreement with both an Agent (marked by the ergative case) and a Patient (in the nominative case; unmarked)
</li>
<li>Direct object ditransitive frame {A-ERG G-LOC T-NOM(1) V-a(A).o(T)}. This frame is associated with caused motion. The verb agrees with both the Agent (marked by the ergative case) and a <em>moving</em> Theme (in the nominative case; unmarked). The Goal is marked with a locative case.
</li>
</ul>

<p>
There are two crucial remarks in relation to these verbal frames: a) arguments are covert most of the time and generally have a low referential density (cf. Bickel 2003). This means that verbal agreement in practice seldom appears along with an overt corresponding semantic role. B) The second point concerns the relation between frames and specific verbs. While a number of verbs only allow an intransitive frame, many verbs that license a 
monotransitive frame can also license an intransitive frame. This is possible through detransitivization, a productive process of valency manipulation (cf. Schikowski 2013a, 21f.; Bickel et al. 2010; Bickel 2011b). For the present dictionary we shall mention the two major strategies thereof.
</p>

<p>I. <strong>S/A detransitivization</strong>. A transitive frame is linked to a detransitivized version: The Agent, normally marked with ergative case, is marked with the nominative (unmarked) and is linked to Subject agreement on the verb. The Object, usually marked by the nominative case and triggering Patient agreement on the verb, is not indexed in the detransitivized frame. The semantic effect of this process is to render the Object non-specific, let us look at two examples:
</p>

<div class="align-me">(5)
<div>mi-kha  nakt-u-ku-ŋ</div>
<div>small-<small>NMLZ</small> ask.for-3P-<small>IND.NPST</small>-1sA</div>
<div>'I will ask for the small (one).' [CLLDCh4R10S10.0264]<br /><br /></div>
</div>

<div class="align-me">(6)
<div>chatpate nak-no ni</div>
<div>chatpate ask.for-<small>IND.NPST</small>[.3sS] <small>EMPH</small></div>
<div>'She is asking for chatpate (a snack of puffed rice).' [CLDLCh3R08S04.268]<br /><br /></div>
</div>

<p>
In example 5. we observe an indexation of both Agent and Patient agreement on the verb. In example 6. we find the same verb licensing a detransitivized frame (instead of the bipersonal form <em>nakt-o-ko</em> 'ask.for-3P-<small>IND.NPST</small>'). The semantic distinction is here expressed in English by the use of a definite article for the Object as opposed to a zero article.
</p>


<p>II. <strong>S/O detransitivization</strong>. Similarly to the aforementioned S/A detransitivization, S/O detransitivization also applies to a transitive frame but this time it entails an outright removal of Agent indexation plus a reinterpretation of the Patient as Subject (that maintains its unmarked nominative case), 
forming an intransitive frame. This detransitivization process has two possible semantic interpretations: it implies either that the event described occurred spontaneously, without an Agent instigator, or that the result continues without the involvement of an Agent. The following examples will illustrate this process:
</p>


<div class="align-me">(7a)
<div>u-laŋ od-a-d-a-ŋs-e raicha thitta-ko</div>
<div>3s.<small>POSS</small>-leg break-<small>PST-COMPL.ITR-PST-PRF-IND.PST</small>[.3sS] MIR one-<small>GEN</small></div>
<div>'This one's leg has broken, it seems.' [CLLDCh1R07S06.323]<br /><br /></div>
</div>

<div class="align-me">(7b)
<div>od-and-u-ku-ŋ ba-go abo ba-go</div>
<div>break-<small>COMPL</small>-3P-<small>IND.NPST</small>-1sA <small>DEM.PROX-NMLZ</small> now <small>DEM.PROX-NMLZ</small></div>
<div>'I will break this one now.' [CLDLCh3R02S04.1225]<br /><br /></div>
</div>

<p>In examples 7a. and b. we encounter the same verb showing two different frames. While 7b. has a monotransitive frame and its corresponding indexing on the verb, in example 7a. we find the verb displaying only Subject agreement with the thematic Patient (the leg) and the thematic Agent is not indexed on the verb. This detransitivization is equivalent to causative-inchoative alternations (unaccusativity) in other languages, however, in Chintang this process is fully productive (cf. Schikowski et al. 2015, pp. 680).<br />
Lastly, there is one point concerning how this detransitization strategy has been integrated into the dictionary. Verbs that allow a transitive frame but also occur in the corpus licensing an intransitive frame (through the productive S/O detransitivizing process) have been integrated into the dictionary as two senses: one with a transitive and another with an intransitive frame. An example would be the verb <em>lett</em> which hence occurs as both 'plant' and 'be.planted'.
</p>
